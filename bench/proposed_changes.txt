 1. Rewrite Conflict Analysis Around Standard 1-UIP
  Current issue: lib/solver.ml:178 repeatedly rebuilds learned-clause vectors during resolution and keeps scanning the reverse trail even after the UIP condition has been reached. That adds a lot of
  allocation and trail scanning. It also appears to produce much larger learned clauses than sat.js, which means more propagation work later.

  Why it matters: CDCL performance depends heavily on learning short asserting clauses. A learned clause should usually be built by marking literals, walking the trail backward until exactly one current-
  level literal remains, then emitting one asserting literal plus literals from lower levels.

  Proposed implementation:

  - Replace the current Learned_clause_state vector-rebuild loop with a mutable conflict analyzer state:
      - seen_by_var : int Vec.Value.t
      - seen_stamp : int
      - learned : int Vec.Value.t
      - path_count : int
      - backjump_level : int64#
      - maybe minimize_seen : int Vec.Value.t later.
  - Start from the failed clause:
      - For each literal in the conflict clause:
          - Ignore decision level 0 literals.
          - Mark its variable as seen.
          - If literal is assigned at current decision level, increment path_count.
          - Otherwise push it into learned.
  - Walk t.trail backward from the end:
      - Skip entries whose variable is not marked.
      - When you hit a seen current-level literal:
          - Clear/consume it.
          - Decrement path_count.
          - If path_count = 0, this trail literal is the UIP. Stop immediately.
          - Otherwise inspect its reason clause and mark/add its literals as above.
  - Build final learned clause as:
      - first literal: negation of UIP assignment, the asserting literal;
      - remaining literals: the lower-level literals collected.
  - Put the literal with the highest non-current decision level in position 1. That makes backjump calculation trivial and makes the clause immediately asserting after backjump.
  - Return both the learned clause and the target backjump level, so second_highest_decision_level does not need to rescan the clause.

  This should replace most of lib/solver.ml:178 through the current backtrack use at lib/solver.ml:836.

  2. Add Learned Clause Minimization
  Current issue: OCaml learned clauses are much larger than JS learned clauses in the sampled runs. The JS solver has a simple minimization pass before adding the nogood: bench/sat.js:345.

  Why it matters: Smaller learned clauses propagate faster, consume less memory, and are usually more useful. This is likely one of the biggest algorithmic gaps.

  Proposed implementation:

  - After building the first-UIP learned clause, run a minimization pass over literals except the asserting literal.
  - For each candidate literal l:
      - If it has no reason, keep it.
      - Otherwise inspect its reason clause.
      - If every non-level-0 variable in that reason is already marked as part of the learned clause or is recursively removable, then drop l.
  - Start with a shallow/minisat-style approximation before doing recursive minimization:
      - Maintain seen_by_var for learned vars.
      - For each candidate reason clause, if all reason vars are already seen or level 0, remove the candidate.
  - Later, add recursive minimization with a separate stamp to avoid cycles and repeated work.

  This can be implemented as a helper near conflict analysis, for example:

  val minimize_learned_clause
    :  t
    -> learned:int Vec.Value.t
    -> asserting_literal:int
    -> int Vec.Value.t

  Keep the first literal fixed. Recompute/select position 1 after minimization.

  3. Stop Scanning All Clauses On Restart
  Current issue: lib/solver.ml:988 clears pending units, unwinds the trail, then scans every clause and calls Clause.unit_literal on each clause. That is a full formula scan on every restart.

  Why it matters: Standard watched-literal CDCL does not need a full clause scan after backtracking/restart. Unassigning variables cannot make a previously satisfied clause falsified; watched literals are
  designed to preserve enough state that future assignments trigger propagation.

  Proposed implementation:

  - Change restart to only:
      - reset conflict counter;
      - backtrack to level 0 or the last assumption level;
      - clear stale pending units.
  - Do not iterate through Clause.Pool.iter.
  - After restart, the main loop will continue with normal watched propagation when new decisions are made.
  - Keep a targeted correctness test:
      - solve a formula requiring propagation after restart;
      - force a restart if needed through a low restart threshold or test-only config;
      - print solver stats/result with expect output.

  The likely replacement is concentrated around lib/solver.ml:988.

  5. Simplify Watched Literal Storage
  Current issue: OCaml keeps watch positions and watch slots on the clause object, and watcher movement goes through lib/solver.ml:426 plus lib/solver.ml:440. JS instead keeps the two watched literals
  physically in clause[0] and clause[1], then swaps array entries during propagation: bench/sat.js:224.

  Why it matters: Propagation is the hottest SAT-solver path. Extra indirection, slot validation, stale-watch cleanup, and clause metadata writes all compound heavily.

  Proposed implementation:

  - Change the clause invariant:
      - for clauses of length >= 2, positions 0 and 1 are the watched literals;
      - moving a watch swaps lits.(watch_idx) with lits.(replacement_pos);
      - watch lists store only clause_idx, not (clause_idx * watch), or store watch_idx only if needed.
  - In propagation:
      - When literal l becomes false, scan the watch list for l.
      - Determine whether l is at clause position 0 or 1.
      - Let the other watched literal be the opposite position.
      - If other watch is true, continue.
      - Scan from index 2 for a non-false literal.
      - If found, swap it into the false watch position and move the clause to the new literal’s watch list.
      - If not found, either propagate the other watch or report conflict.
  - Remove watch0_pos, watch1_pos, watch0_slot, watch1_slot from Clause.t if the new watcher representation no longer needs them.

  This is a larger refactor, so do it after conflict analysis and restart cleanup. It needs strong expect tests for propagation, backtracking, learned clauses, and clause deletion.

  6. Add Phase Saving And Improve Branching
  Current issue: lib/vsids.ml:184 chooses a literal directly from per-literal activity. There is no obvious phase saving. SAT instances often benefit a lot from choosing the last successful polarity for a
  variable.

  Why it matters: The variable choice decides where to search; the phase choice decides whether the solver quickly reconstructs a satisfying assignment. VSIDS without phase saving can churn.

  Proposed implementation:

  - Split decision selection into:
      - choose variable by activity;
      - choose polarity by saved phase.
  - Add saved_phase : Bitset.t or bool option Vec.Value.t to solver state.
  - Whenever assigning a literal, update saved phase for that variable.
  - On decision:
      - choose highest-activity unassigned variable;
      - use saved phase if present;
      - otherwise use a default phase, probably false for CNF with many negative pairwise constraints, or initialize by literal occurrence counts.
  - Simplify Vsids to score variables rather than literals, or keep literal scores but choose variable by max of positive/negative score and polarity by saved phase.

  This change belongs mostly in lib/vsids.ml:91 and lib/solver.ml:879.

  7. Re-tune Restarts And Clause Deletion After The Above
  Current issue: clause simplification/deletion runs every 2500 solver iterations: lib/solver.ml:1027. Iterations are not the best unit; conflicts are usually the right clock. Also deletion before learned
  clauses are high quality can throw away useful clauses or spend time sorting weak clauses.

  Why it matters: Restarts and deletion amplify whatever conflict learning produces. Tune them after learned clauses become smaller and cheaper.

  Proposed implementation:

  - Trigger clause database reduction by conflict count, not recursive solver-loop iterations.
  - Always keep:
      - binary clauses;
      - clauses with low LBD, especially LBD <= 2;
      - clauses currently used as reasons.
  - Reduce only learned clauses above a size/LBD threshold.
  - Sort candidates by a combined key such as (lbd, activity, size) rather than activity alone.
  - Make restart/deletion knobs configurable so benchmarks can compare:
      - no restarts;
      - Luby restarts;
      - geometric restarts;
      - different deletion intervals.

  Expected sequence: first make learned clauses good, then reduce restart scan cost, then tune deletion. Tuning before that will mostly hide the larger structural costs.
